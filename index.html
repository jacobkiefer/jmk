Technical Skills

Performed control system analysis, verification and validation support, and stability analysis. To address the nonlinear, time varying dynamics of a launch vehicle, a stability analysis technique is to incrementally adjust the gain and/or time delay in a time domain simulation until the system exhibits unstable behavior. This technique was implemented on a simulated ascent trajectory with varying degrees of stability with an adaptive control algorithm active and inactive. The results show that the adaptive algorithm generally provides an additional 6 dB of gain margin.

SLS Flight Controls

Landing on uprepared, sloping surfaces or a moving ship are some of the greatest challenges faced by rotorcraft today. Most rotorcraft have severely limited landing site options. Moreover, landings in complex terrain are performed very slowly. Robotic landing gear (RGL) can be used to overcome these challenges and enable revolutionary operational capabilities. For example, the damage caused by impact during a hard landing can be mitigated with the RLG. It can soften the impact by acting as a shock absorber with a large stroke compared with traditional landing gear. My research showed that the RLG can reduce peak loads during landing by up to 90% compared to a convential landing gear. See publication

A design of experiments (DOE) approach was used to investigate factors affecting the synthesis of fluorescent polystyrene latex microspheres (PSLs) for use in wind tunnel measurement techniques such as PIV and LDV. The ability to measure temperature simultaneously with velocity can provide a greater understanding of flow physics. To this end, PSLs were doped with a temperature dependant fluorescent dye. The DOE was used to gain insight about what effect different factors had on the particle synthesis process, as well as find the best configuration to produce PSLs of the correct size with the fluorescent dye incorporated well into the particles. Although not identical to the motion of nature's swimmers and flyers, simple harmonic oscillations of cantilever-like structures can provide efficient low-power solutions for applications ranging from thermal management to propulsion. In order to quantify their true potential, the flow field and corresponding thrust must be better understood. To this end, a cantilever was actuated at its resonant frequency via a piezoelectric patch mounted near its base. Flow fields and thrust were quantified and used to motivate future configurations that could greatly enhance thrust capabilities by directing the flow downstream in a more efficient manner.
